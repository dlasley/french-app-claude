## Points de départ - The Olympic Games

### Historical Information (page 55)
Pierre de Coubertin founded the first modern Olympic Games in 1892. They took place in Athens, Greece, where 241 athletes participated, representing 14 nations. In today's Games, approximately 15,000 athletes participate, representing 206 delegations. Coubertin also designed the original flag, which made its debut in 1920. The flame, a symbol of the link between the modern and ancient Games, was added in 1928. In addition to the official flag and motto, "Faster, higher, stronger" (Plus vite, plus haut, plus fort), the Olympics have three official languages: French, English, and the language of the host country. Today we also celebrate the Paralympic Games and the Youth Olympic Games.

## Culturelle - Paris (pages 2.1, 2.2, 4.2)

Called the **ville lumière** (*city of lights*), Paris is one of the three most cosmopolitan cities in the world, along with London and New York. Measuring 40 square miles with 2.3 million inhabitants and 20 **arrondissements** (districts), it is compact enough to be visited on foot! Paris is famous for its iconic monuments (**la tour Eiffel, Notre-Dame**), its museums (**le musée du Louvre** and **le musée d'Orsay**), and its contemporary buildings (**le Centre Pompidou, la Bibliothèque nationale de France, la Philharmonie de Paris**). **The Seine** is the river that divides Paris into the business district on the **rive droite** (*right bank*), and the intellectual and artistic district on the **rive gauche** (*left bank*).

Paris's unique architecture includes 335 bridges, the wide avenue of the **Champs-Élysées**, gardens (**Tuileries, Luxembourg**), and public parks and squares. Paris is known for its fashion, culture, and intellectual scenes, which attract many tourists. Today Paris reflects a young and multi-ethnic culture with theme cafes, modern film libraries, the three-mile **Coulée verte** elevated linear park, and a new modern green space in the area of **l'axe République-Bastille-Porte Dorée**. New spaces are being created to make Paris a **cité numérique** (*smart city*), an urban development concept.

## Structure de la langue - Subject Pronouns (page 1.2)

### French Subject Pronouns
- **Je** = I
- **Tu** = you (singular)
- **il/elle/on** = he/she/one
- **Nous** = we
- **Vous** = you (plural, formal)
- **ils/elles** = they

### Usage Notes
To talk to or about people, you can use subject pronouns to replace their names. A subject pronoun refers to the person (or thing) that completes the action of a sentence, and is either singular (referring to one person) or plural (referring to more than one person).

## Tu vs. Vous (pages 1.2, 4.1)

The subject pronouns **tu** and **vous** both mean *you* but are used in different ways. When you talk to one person,

### Use *tu* (the familiar form) with:
- a friend
- a person your own age
- a close relative
- a child
- a pet

### Use *vous* (the formal form) with:
- an acquaintance
- a person older than you
- a distant relative
- an adult you don't know
- a person of authority, such as a teacher or doctor

When talking to more than one person, always use **vous**.

Example: Anne, Lucie, Amir, comment allez-vous? (*Anne, Lucie, Amir, how are you?*)

3. To cry
4. To sleep

### French Infinitives Explained
A verb expresses an action or a state of being. The basic form of a verb is called the **infinitive**. In English, it consist of two words: a verb with the word *to* before it (to help, to swim, to like, etc.). The infinitive form is found in the end vocabulary of this textbook and in French dictionaries. Many French infinitives end in **-er**, such as **présenter, aider, nager, aimer, manger, jouer**, and **plonger**.

**En français on peut conjugate les verbs en trois forms, *er, ir et re*.** (In French you can conjugate verbs in three forms, -er, -ir, and -re.)
